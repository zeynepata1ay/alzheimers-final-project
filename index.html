Global Insights Into Alzheimer’s Risk Factors

INTRODUCTION

Alzheimer's disease is a progressive neurodegenerative disorder leading to severe cognitive decline and loss of independence. Symptoms include severe memory loss, poor planning, and loss of physical control. It is unfortunately quite common. Affecting 6.9 million Americans, its prevalence is rising, potentially due to controllable environmental factors (NIH Alzheimers and Environmental Factors). Further, recent scientific discoveries have shown our previous theory of Alzheimer's, the amyloid cascade hypothesis, is incorrect. And in the literature, the specific cause of Alzheimer's is still poorly understood. Highly efficacious Alzheimer's medications remain to be seen. However, it may be possible to reduce the odds of developing the disease via lifestyle changes. For our project, we examined biological features from our dataset such as age, Body Mass Index (BMI), the Genetic Risk Factor (APOE-ε4 allele). We also looked at environmental factors including physical activity, smoker status, and sleep quality to evaluate if the disease was predominantly affected by nature or nurture. After combining all these factors, we sought to provide actionable recommendations for preventing Alzheimer’s disease.

DATA

The dataset chosen for this project is the “Alzheimer’s Prediction Dataset (Global)” sourced from Kaggle. This dataset is comprehensive, containing 74,283 records collected from 20 countries. It offers valuable insights into Alzheimer’s disease risk factors, including demographic, lifestyle, medical, and genetic variables. The dataset reflects real-world disparities across regions by incorporating a biased distribution, so conclusions about risk factors may be less applicable to countries less represented. It includes more than 2,000 observations and over 10 features, with a mix of categorical and continuous attributes. These characteristics enabled diverse analyses and visualizations, allowing our group to explore factors and present findings effectively.

SUMMARY & FINDINGS

In this project, we sought to answer which factors played the greatest role in predicting Alzheimer’s Disease diagnoses. As this disease affects millions of people, we believe it's important to provide actionable advice for its prevention. Our exploration of correlation factors and genetic risk populations showed us that having a family history of Alzheimer’s and APOE-ε4 allele were two clear linear factors in Alzheimer’s prediction. Along with biological factors which are out of patients’ control, we also looked into the impacts of lifestyle habits or the question of “Nature vs. Nurture”. Nature factors had a 22.82% influence on predicting a positive Alzheimer’s diagnosis. Factors like age, genetic risk, or family history. Nurture factors had a 77.18% influence. This data lends credence that personal action and circumstance has a significant impact on Alzheimers. Our findings have shown that age and genetics remain the clearest factors for Alzheimer’s diagnosis. However the impact of lifestyle choices should not be overlooked. In terms of body health: BMI, physical activity, dietary habits and others predict Alzheimer’s diagnosis. Therefore it is efficacious to monitor BMI and make healthy food choices. Activities like going outside with friends or sports are two effective ways to promote physical activity and socialization. Further, higher education may also lower your risk. In conclusions we find that risk factors mitigation can enable individuals to avoid or delay Alzheimer’s disease despite some factors being beyond patient control.
